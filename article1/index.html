Fleet Fuel Management Costs Are Rising — Here's What the Data Shows

An in-depth look at how fuel expenses are reshaping fleet operations across the United States

For fleet operators across the United States, fuel remains the single largest variable expense on the balance sheet. Industry analysts estimate that fuel accounts for 15 to 30 percent of total fleet operating costs, a figure that has steadily climbed alongside volatile diesel and gasoline prices over the past three years. With U.S. logistics expenses reaching $2.3 trillion in 2023 alone, the pressure on fleet managers to contain fuel spending has never been more intense.

The Scale of the Fleet Fuel Market

The global fuel card market reached an estimated $877 billion to $1.01 trillion in 2025, with projections pointing toward $960 billion to $1.17 trillion by the end of 2026. Those growth figures represent compound annual growth rates between 10.7% and 14.2%, depending on the research source. In North America specifically, the commercial fuel card market was valued at $201.6 billion in 2025, reflecting the outsized role that American fleets play in the global logistics economy.

Per-Gallon Savings Add Up Quickly

One of the most compelling arguments for fleet fuel card adoption is the per-gallon discount structure. Universal fleet cards typically offer savings between 3 and 15 cents per gallon at participating stations, while specialized over-the-road trucking cards deliver significantly higher discounts — reaching 45 to 57 cents per gallon at in-network truck stops.

For perspective, an owner-operator burning 20,000 gallons of diesel annually would save approximately $5,000 per year at a conservative 25-cent-per-gallon discount. A mid-size fleet consuming 384,000 gallons annually could see annual savings exceeding $57,000 — capital that gets redirected toward vehicle maintenance, driver retention, or fleet expansion. Companies like Fleet Fuel Cards help fleet operators identify the right card programs to maximize those per-gallon savings.

Beyond the Pump: Bundled Cost Reduction

Modern fleet fuel card programs extend well beyond simple per-gallon discounts. Many programs now include maintenance cost reductions of up to 30% on vehicle servicing, tires, and truck washes. Some providers offer up to $40 per tire in additional discounts, a meaningful benefit for fleets running heavy equipment on long-haul routes.

The integration of telematics with fuel card data has created a new category of fleet intelligence. Managers can now correlate fuel consumption with route efficiency, driver behavior, and vehicle health in a single dashboard. This convergence of data streams is driving a broader shift toward AI-driven fleet optimization, with the fleet management technology market expected to grow at compound rates between 8.4% and 15.5% through 2034.

Fraud Prevention and Spend Control

Fuel fraud remains a persistent concern for fleet operators managing distributed teams of drivers. Fleet fuel cards address this through multiple control layers: per-transaction spending limits, PIN verification, time-of-day restrictions, and geographic constraints that flag purchases outside approved routes. Advanced programs now incorporate AI-based anomaly detection and blockchain-backed transaction transparency to further reduce unauthorized spend.

The shift away from cash-based fueling eliminates one of the oldest sources of fleet shrinkage. When every transaction is digitally recorded, auditable, and tied to a specific driver and vehicle, the opportunities for fuel theft or receipt manipulation drop dramatically. This level of visibility is one reason the U.S. freight index saw a 3.4% increase in fuel card adoption in 2024, as carriers sought tighter financial controls during a period of margin pressure.

What Fleet Operators Should Consider

Selecting the right fuel card for fleet operations depends on several factors: fleet size, typical route geography, fuel type requirements, and whether drivers need access to universal station networks or can operate within specialized truck stop networks. Universal cards like Voyager and WEX cover 95 to 97 percent of U.S. gas stations, while specialized OTR cards offer deeper discounts at fewer locations.

With fuel costs projected to remain elevated through 2026 and fleet operating margins under continued pressure, the ROI case for structured fuel card programs has rarely been stronger. Fleet operators that leverage the right combination of per-gallon discounts, bundled maintenance savings, and data-driven route optimization are positioning themselves for measurable competitive advantages in an increasingly cost-sensitive market.
